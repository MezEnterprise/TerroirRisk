A wine critic gives a number after dinner. We give a number from the atmosphere — soil moisture, thermal range, growing-degree days — measured by satellite and reanalysis, the same for everyone, audited against the vine's own physiology. No palate. No invitation list. No revision after the auction.

01

A 100-point score is a private judgment sold as a public fact. Two critics disagree by 5 points on the same bottle. The market moves anyway.

02

The growing season is not subjective. The clay holds water or it doesn't. September nights are cold or they aren't. That signal is recorded, free, and gridded over every appellation on earth.

03

We fit one physical driver per regime — out-of-sample, leave-one-vintage-out — and publish the prediction before the vintage is tasted. Then we wait.

Higher soil moisture at harvest means a wetter, more diluted Merlot on Pomerol's water-holding blue clay — and a lower score. The sign is negative by physics, declared before the scores were seen. We publish a band, not a single point: the model's out-of-sample MAE is ±1.84, so the honest claim is the interval, and the direction relative to the appellation mean.

On critics and noise. We also fitted the same model against Wine Advocate pure scores (hand-extracted from subscription). The physical signal still holds — same negative sign, same driver — but out-of-sample accuracy drops: R²oos 0.171 vs 0.289, MAE 2.86 vs 1.84. Parker's individual scores carry a critic-variance premium of roughly ±1 point that the atmosphere cannot predict. The Wine-Searcher aggregate filters that noise. That difference is not a flaw in the physics — it is a measurement of how much of a score is weather, and how much is palate.

Who is behind this

A physicist and an AIS-certified sommelier, working solo, against the grain. One side knows reanalysis grids and out-of-sample validation; the other knows what's actually in the glass. Neither sells scores.

The premise is adversarial on purpose: every prediction is a falsifiable claim, time-stamped in public, that the establishment's number can be anticipated from the weather it pretends to transcend — or can't, which is itself the finding.
